Overview

The goal of this project was to study how text based forums enable academic discusions. The scope was narrowed down to two popular platforms Disocrd and Slack

This project was done in two teams of three, each focusing on Discord and Slack respectively while combining and studying research outcomes from both wherever necessary. My focus was on Discord and how Discord enabled academic collaboration.

Initial Observations

We orbited around studying how online communities function in a text based environment. We considered platforms like Discord, Slack, Reddit, Imgur, Twitch, Linkedin, Instagram and Facebook and found out Slack and Discord pair was the most similar and comparable among others and decided to observe those two.

Discord

  • First Impression :
    • Gives visceral feeling of “Friendship”
    • User logs into their account and then is free to select servers
    • Users use their IGN (In game name) or ‘gamertag’ as their Discord name
    • Generates unique message for joining inplace of the plain “You have Joined”
  • Chat :
    • Chats informal, fun and have gaming lingo to them
    • The communication is through two channels, Voice and Text.
    • Users quickly transition between text and voice chat, often use it in
    • conjunction to share information that could be needed later
    • Bots are an important part of discord server but they seem to be absent in this
    • server
    • Emote reactions to chat messages used frequently
  • Roles :
    • ‘Roles’ can be assigned to users by admin/owner or users with admin powers.
    • Roles are used to label users and give or take away administrative power
    • Username color changes according to their given role
  • Channel :
    • Each channel is an individual conversation
    • Channels can be hidden, users with specific roles have clearance for certain
    • channels
    • Important messages are ‘pinned’ to channel
  • Misc Features :
    • Accessibility, Discord provides TTS by for each message as "Speak
    • Message" in “option”
    • ’Search’ tool is simple and handy
    • User profiles are informal. Amateur users can use it to send friend requests
    • and private DMs. Expert users will add nicknames, and additional notes in the
    • "notes section" to help remember who is who. Email is not publicly visible.
    • New study server can be created within two clicks with server templates
  • Meetings/Discussions/Academic Conversation :
    • Discussions flow all around the topic with ‘memes’ and jokes in between, it is
    • more of a chat and less of work

Slack

  • First Impression :
    • Gives visceral feeling of “Formality”
    • User logs into separate workplaces (servers). Switching servers is clumsy
    • Users use their real names or sometimes nicknames as their slack username
  • Chat :
    • Chats are wordy, formal and have a corporate lingo to them
  • Roles :
    • no concept of roles
  • Channel :
    • Gives feeling of privacy
    • Channels can be locked and hidden
    • Important announcements and messages are ‘pinned’ to channel
  • Misc Features :
    • ‘Threads’ is very handy in continuing parallely branching convos within one channel. Used frequently.
    • ’Search’ tool is simple and handy
    • User profiles allow DM and call but the registered Email is publicly visible
    • Meetings/Discussions/Academic Conversation :
    • Discussion feel focused, to the point and productive

Summary and Research Questions My observations were based on three key points, the ease of creating or joining a community, ways to maintain and moderate the community and lastly the ease of navigating through discussions and posts. Creating a server in Discord was faster and took less effort than creating a workspace in slack. The presence of ‘roles’ definitely helps in moderating large communities whereas absence of it in slack suggests slack is meant for smaller scale, ‘team collaboration’. The text chat structure does feel disjointed in Discord whereas in Slack it feels organised and connected to the purpose of the workspace. As for the voice chat Discord has voice channels which allow conversations to move seamlessly from text to voice. Both chats had similar technical content but the lingo in which they were being discussed was different, which made me think about the ways in which a culture associated with a platform influences its user’s way of communicating. The feeling I got while using Slack was that of a closed space similar to an office cubicle, whereas Discord made me feel like I was at a Carnival, chaotic but every feature has a purpose and users were having ‘fun’ with them. At the time I thought it would be interesting to discuss how others felt about it and what qualities associated with respective platforms induced that feeling.

Slack and Discord are aimed at different audiences but both platforms have similar if not same core features, a research question could be drawn out on how same features are being implemented differently ? Another broader question can be, despite having the same underlying technology and common features, why is Slack and Discord being used for two different purposes?

Contextual Inquiry

We followed Rapid Contextual Design guide by Holtzblatt, Wendell, and Wood and executed it in digital form using Miro.

The contextual inquiry was collection of in-person and online semi-structured interviews. The interviews aimed at understanding users expectations from an app like this and what they think is the biggest hurdle that will keep them from using such an app regularly.

There were 16 interviews in total used for affinity mapping.

User Testing

We approached the same user whom we had interviewed for contextual inquiry. Every User was tasked with navigating the app based on use cases.


Users did not understand the working of the Life Score feature
Users wanted to customize the recipes
Users had trouble finding the button to restock utilized items
Users wanted to see expiry of items without having to go through all the text

Changes made based on above feedback

Using icons to better depict data
Change of visual language of buttons : Making the buttons more visible.
Life Score: Impact of the life score on every meal rather than just the overall score.
Customization of the Recipes: Customization option for recipes to give more control to the user.
Button for adding utilized items for restock
Manual entry button: Manual entry button after scanning of receipt to give users more options.
Color coding for “number of days until expiry” : eg. 1-2 days in red, 3-4 days in yellow, >4 green.
